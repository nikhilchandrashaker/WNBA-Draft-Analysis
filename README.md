# Finding Hidden Gems: 25 Years of WNBA Draft Value and Player Success

**Research question:** Which factors best predict WNBA draft success, and which
franchises consistently outperform expectations in the draft?

This project evaluates **draft picks**, not just players — building an expected-value
curve for every draft slot (1997–2022), then measuring players, colleges, and franchises
against that baseline. The goal is an answer to "how should a GM draft?", not a
descriptive Kaggle-style writeup.

## Data

`data/wnba_draft_raw.csv` — original source, 1,064 picks, 1997–2022, 14 columns.
No player position is available; all analysis here is positionless.

`data/wnba_draft_clean.csv` — cleaned/feature-engineered version produced by
`01_data_cleaning.ipynb`. See `data/data_dictionary.csv` for full column definitions.
Key additions: `draft_era`, `round` (derived from actual picks-per-team per year, not
assumed), `lottery_pick`, `franchise` (relocation-adjusted lineage), `is_international`,
`never_played`, `career_bucket`.

`data/draft_pick_value.csv` — output of `03_draft_value.ipynb`: expected win shares,
bust rate, and star rate for every draft slot, both raw and LOWESS-smoothed.

`data/draft_steal_scores.csv` — output of `04_draft_efficiency.ipynb`: every player's
Draft Steal Score (career value minus what their slot predicted), flagged for whether
their draft class has had enough time (2021-2022 excluded from career-value judgments).

`data/franchise_draft_scorecard.csv` — output of `05_team_drafting.ipynb`: every
franchise's average steal score, steal/bust rate, and Return on Draft Capital.

`data/feature_importance.csv`, `data/model_comparison.csv` — output of
`06_predicting_success.ipynb`: Linear Regression / Random Forest / Gradient Boosting
comparison and feature importances for predicting career win shares, games, and years played.

`data/gems_*.csv` — output of `07_hidden_gems.ipynb`: best steals ever, most underrated
draft classes, best second-round picks, most overlooked colleges, international success
stories.

`dashboard/index.html` — the interactive dashboard. Single self-contained file, no server
required; open it directly in a browser. Reads `dashboard/data.js` (generated by
`08_dashboard.ipynb`).

`reports/Draft Value Report.pdf` — the executive report. 14 pages: Executive Summary,
Historical Draft Trends, the Draft Value Curve, Team Drafting Ability, Hidden Gems,
Busts, Predictive Model, and Recommendations.

## Headline findings so far

- **The pick #1-5 gap is real and steep**, then flattens hard: expected career win shares
  drop from ~22 (pick 1) to under 1 by pick ~14. Past the top of Round 1, differences
  between nearby picks are mostly noise, not signal.
- **Biggest career steal ever (by this metric):** Tamika Catchings, picked #3 in 2001,
  produced ~76 win shares above what a #3 pick historically predicts.
- **Best value found outside Round 1:** Taj McWilliams-Franklin, picked #32 (Round 3) in
  1999, and Tammy Sutton-Brown, picked #18 (Round 2) in 2001 — both massively outproduced
  picks in their range.
- **Franchise drafting is genuinely uneven.** By average steal score per pick, Connecticut
  Sun (including its Orlando Miracle lineage) and the Houston Comets rank at the top;
  Portland Fire (small sample, only 3 draft classes before folding) ranks last.
- **Draft position explains the large majority of career outcome variance** (~72-74%
  feature importance across Gradient Boosting and Random Forest), with college program
  pedigree a clear but distant second (~14%), and international status/draft era trailing
  further behind. Held-out test R² for predicting career win shares: ~0.40 — genuinely
  noisy, which is the whole premise of a "hidden gems" project.

## Key modeling decisions worth knowing before reading further

- **Zero-fill, don't drop.** A player who never played in the WNBA counts as a `0` in
  every pick-value calculation. Dropping them would silently condition the "expected
  value of a pick" on making the league at all, which defeats the purpose.
- **Round numbers are derived, not assumed.** Draft structure changed a lot over 25 years
  (4 rounds/16 teams in the early 2000s down to 3 rounds/12 teams today). `round` is
  computed from the actual number of teams picking in a given year.
- **Franchise lineage vs. team-at-the-time.** `team` keeps the name active when the pick
  was made; `franchise` collapses relocations (Utah Starzz → San Antonio Stars → Las
  Vegas Aces is one lineage) so Phase 5's team-evaluation isn't artificially fragmented.
- **`former` is not a clean "international" flag.** It's also populated for US college
  players who played in the old ABL before the 1997 draft (e.g. Dawn Staley). The
  reliable international signal is `college` being null.
- **The pick-value curve is LOWESS-smoothed**, because raw per-pick averages are very
  noisy beyond ~pick 36 (only 3-4 historical instances per slot late in the draft).
